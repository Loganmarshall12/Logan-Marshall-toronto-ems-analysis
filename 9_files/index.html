Introduction & Background

Emergency Medical Services play a vital role in reducing fatalities and severe injuries resulting from motor vehicle collisions, especially when rapid response can make the difference between life and death. In urban environments like Toronto, accessibility to timely EMS care is not uniform across all neighborhoods.

This project explores spatial patterns in EMS accessibility, with a specific focus on severe collisions that involve fatalities or major injuries using 2023 data. The analysis utilizes a combination of road network data, EMS station locations, and collision data for high-severity incidents.

Figure 1: Spatial Distribution of KSI Collisions and EMS Stations in Toronto, 2023. This baseline map shows the relationship between collision locations (green dots) and EMS station locations (heart symbols) across the city, revealing initial patterns of clustering in downtown areas.

Research Questions

  1. How effectively does the current EMS infrastructure service all areas of Toronto?
  2. Where are the major MVC hotspots in Toronto, and do they align with EMS coverage zones?
  3. Are there geographic or socioeconomic disparities in EMS response times and serious motor vehicle collisions across neighborhoods?
  4. How could strategic modifications to EMS station placement improve emergency accessibility and reduce response times?

This work addresses a significant gap in Canadian urban emergency services research by focusing specifically on Toronto, using spatial analysis to assess the relationship between EMS service areas and high-risk collision zones, while also accounting for socioeconomic variation.

Methodology Overview

Spatial Distribution Mapping

Visualized the spatial distribution of EMS stations and KSI collisions to establish baseline understanding of service reach and collision hotspots.

Service Area Analysis

Generated 6-minute, 8-minute, and 10-minute travel time zones using Network Analyst tool, representing different EMS response performance thresholds.

Kernel Density Estimation

Performed KDE analysis with 1500-meter search radius to identify areas with highest concentration of severe motor vehicle collisions.

Rush Hour Analysis

Conducted secondary network analysis using Monday 5 PM traffic conditions to account for peak hour travel time variations.

Results & Analysis

The analysis revealed significant spatial patterns in EMS accessibility and collision distribution across Toronto. The most striking finding occurs in the downtown core, where the highest concentration of severe collisions aligns with service zones outside of the desired 0-to-6-minute response range.

Figure 6: EMS Coverage vs. High-Risk Collision Areas, Toronto, Ontario 2023. This critical overlay demonstrates the spatial mismatch between areas of highest collision density (dark purple) and optimal EMS response zones, particularly in the downtown core.

üéØ Critical Findings

Downtown Vulnerability Despite high EMS station density, downtown areas show significant KSI collision concentration outside optimal response zones due to traffic congestion.
Service Coverage Gaps Multiple areas across Toronto fall outside the 6-8 minute response threshold, particularly in central and peripheral locations.
Rush Hour Impact Peak traffic conditions significantly expand EMS accessibility gaps, particularly affecting downtown and central Toronto coverage.
Socioeconomic Disparities Lower-income neighborhoods experience both higher collision density and reduced EMS accessibility, creating compounding vulnerabilities.

The kernel density analysis clearly shows collision clustering concentrated in downtown and central/southern Toronto, with the majority of high-density areas falling within service zones that exceed the critical 6-minute response threshold.

Discussion & Implications

This study provides meaningful insight into the spatial distribution of EMS accessibility and KSI collisions in Toronto, with significant implications for emergency response planning and transportation equity. The identification of service gaps, especially in areas with high collision density and lower average income, highlights the need for targeted infrastructure improvements.

The downtown core presents a particular challenge - despite having nearby EMS stations, it shows significant concentration of KSI collisions outside the optimal 0-to-6-minute response range. This reflects research emphasizing that survival rates in trauma incidents significantly decline when EMS response exceeds six minutes.

The overlap between high-density collision clusters and lower-income neighborhoods supports the transportation equity aspect of the analysis, revealing systemic vulnerabilities in emergency access. Prior studies have demonstrated that disadvantaged communities often face slower EMS response, compounding their risk during emergencies.

‚ö†Ô∏è Study Limitations

  • Road network may not reflect real-world variability (construction, weather delays)
  • Single-year collision data doesn't account for long-term trend fluctuations

  • Some identified gaps correspond to parks or specialized areas (airports) with existing emergency services

Future work should integrate real-time traffic conditions, multi-year collision data, and detailed land-use analysis to refine recommendations and ensure implementation feasibility.
